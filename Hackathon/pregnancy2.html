Pregnancy & Abuse : Planning A Safe Child Birth

Childbirth is an exciting yet appalling event for many first time parents. While there are many ways to prepare yourself for your child's birth, everyone has a different interpretation of the ideal birth, and these measures can vary from person to person. Some people develop a birth plan to describe what they'd like to do during and immediately after birth. When you are still worried about the effect or role of an abusive partner during the birth, a birth plan should provide protection measures.

Remember the allies that you'll have available during birth when you build this strategy. If you intend to give birth in a hospital, it is possible that physicians and nurses will be present for most of your delivery cycle. You can have a midwife present whether you give birth at a birthing center or at home. You would have been able to tell these practitioners of your concerns about the violence, depending on your prenatal care choices. If not, it might be possible to contact the practitioners in advance and to prepare a few things to add to your birth plan for protection. You may even have a specialist for help at birth, like a doula.While doulas may not have domestic violence training or help someone who is experiencing harassment, you will still be able to reach out to them during your work for additional support. Although doula expenses may not be covered by insurance, some doulas may provide pro bono or sliding-scale services. If you don't have a birth doula, you may want to find a member or friend of your family to take on the position of labor support. Bear in mind when deciding who to ask, whether you might want someone to schedule safety for you as opposed to you.

Childbirth requires tremendous energy and concentration. Even if you are planning a c-section ahead of time, this is a major surgery that deserves full attention. It 's important that you can completely access your reserves without having distractions, no matter what your birth plan is. When you feel like your abusive partner or ex-partner is trying to keep you from taking the required steps to get a healthy and stress-free birth, consider adding tactics to your birth plan to refocus and energize you. For different people different strategies work, so practice these beforehand to see what is most effective for you.These include movement exercises, breathing exercises, guided meditation or relaxation narratives, listening or singing to music and repeating positive affirmations. The key is that you are able to stay relaxed and positive.

When you have left the relationship, or go into work when your partner is not present, you will agree that the best thing for you and your child is to keep them from finding out you are giving birth. You will be able to do that by alerting your work support person only when you go to work, and making sure they know not to share the information with someone else. When deciding when you are going to give birth, you will want to remember if your husband or ex knows your due date, and whether they may try to contact hospitals, birth centers or your support network in the region to try and find you.Once you determine a plan, let the staff at the place where you give birth know to alert you if someone tries looking for you, as well as to not provide any information about your presence or status. Give them a picture of your partner/ex, and ask that staff alert you if anyone matching their description is reported in the area.You may want to take a taxi or let a friend or family member drive you in a car that your partner / ex won't know. Tell your work staff to search the parking lot as you leave the facility and make sure your partner / ex isn't waiting for you. While it is understandable that you would like to share your birth information with social networks, consider safety before sharing notifications or details. Friends of friends can also show pictures online, even if the abuser is blocked. If family and friends are visiting, ask them to wait until after you've returned home to share any pictures they take of you or the baby.

As part of your birth plan protection steps, you will be able to decide a code word to use for your doctor , nurse, midwife, doula or other labor help to warn them if you feel unsafe and want your abuser to be removed from the room. You may also have your partner staying with a relative or family member to keep them from interrupting your attention during childbirth. Whatever circumstances or needs you may have, The Hotline is available to help you, whether it is identifying local options or national resources that can improve your safety, developing a personalized safety plan that helps you maintain your childbirth reserves, or providing emotional support and validation during the last pregnancy phase.
