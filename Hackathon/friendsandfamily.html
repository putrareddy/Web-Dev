Help For Friends And Family

Were you worried that you suffer violence from someone you care about? You may have found other warning signs, including:

  • Their partner brings them down before others
  • They are always preoccupied with making their partner angry
  • We are making excuses for the actions of their partner
  • Their partner is extremely jealous or possessive
  • They have unexplained marks or injuries
  • They 're sad or nervous or you find personality changes
  • They’ve stopped spending time with friends and family

If someone you love is being abused, then knowing what to do can be so difficult. Your impulse may be to "get" them out of the relationship, but it's not that easy. After all, there are many explanations why people remain in abusive relationships, and for a survivor to leave can be a very risky time.

Abuse is about power and control, and understanding how you can empower them to make their own decisions is one of the most important ways you can support a individual in an abusive relationship. Besides, you can provide help in various ways

Recognize they are in a very challenging and distressing situation, be respectful and listen

Let them know they are not to blame for the violence. Reassure them that they are not alone, and that there is help and assistance out there. We may have trouble thinking about the violence. Let them know you 're still there to help anytime they need it. What they desperately need is someone who can trust and listen.

Be dismissive

Respect the actions of your relative, or family member. There are plenty of explanations why victims remain in abusive relations. They can go away several times and return to the relationship. Don't condemn or attempt to blame their actions. During those times they'll need your support even more.

When they end the partnership, continue to support them

Because the relationship was abusive, once it's over your friend or family member can always feel depressed and lonely. They will need time to mourn the loss of the relationship and will need your help at that point in particular.

Encourage them to take part in events outside of friends and family relationships

Support is important and the more people who care for them feel secure, the easier it will be for them to take the necessary steps to get away from their abusive partner and to remain healthy. Please remember to call the hotline to find local support groups and details about staying healthy.

Aid them devise a security strategy

Check our details on building a security plan wherever you are in your relationship — if you want to stay, prepare to leave, or have already left.

Mind you can't 'rescue them'

While it is hard to see someone you care for being hurt, they are eventually the one who has to make the choices on what they want to do. It is important that you support them whatever they decide, and help them find a path to stability and peace.

Helping Your Teen

Important points to remember when helping your teen are:

  • Consider, listen and be respectful of what your child tells you. Seek not to criticize them, even though you don't understand or agree with their decisions. It can make those people feel worse.
  • Don't share information on social networking sites about these. Never use sites such as Twitter or foursquare to announce their present location or where they are standing. Their partner will be using your post to locate them. Brush up your information security know-how.
  • Let them know for themselves. Leaving a relationship that is unhealthy or abusive can be difficult, and even risky. Stop criticizing the remarks or belittling them. Usually abusive partners routinely bring their victims down, so your loved one's self-esteem will already be weak.
  • Though it can be difficult to help, don't give up. They need to know more than anything that they can trust you and count on you.
  • Don't hold them off from seeing their abusive partner. It can cause them to feel as if they need to keep secrets from you, as well as feel as if they are being taken away from them in making decisions.
Helping Your Co-Worker

When anyone encounters violence at home, it is highly possible that the consequences of the violence will spill over into the work environment. You may note changes in their at-work behaviour, which may mean something is wrong. Use, for example:

  • Excessive lateness or unexplained absences
  • Unexplained injuries or bruising
  • Lack of concentration/being preoccupied more often
  • Sensitivity about home life or hints of trouble at home
  • Disruptive phone calls or personal visits from their partner

What do you do?

  • Follow the feeling and if you believe you should speak to them about what could happen, do so. The worst thing that could happen is they don't want to talk-and even then at least they know you care.
  • Make sure to contact them in a discreet way, without interruptions at any time and location. Recall being non-judgmental while addressing domestic abuse with your coworker. They may be embarrassed by the situation and maybe you are the first person they tell.
  • Introduce them to the security guard, or travel with them to meet the security guard if they would like assistance. Keeping a security guard in the office loop can help deter your coworker's abuser from stopping by, make sure your coworker is securely escorted to and from office space, and more.
  • Listen about what they have to say if your coworker opens up to you about the violence. Your job is not to solve the problem for them-sometimes it can be more helpful to listen. Perhaps you want to pass on any details to them. If it feels appropriate, forward The Hotline number.
  • Ask if they want to develop a work environment health plan. Ask what they want you to do if the office will call or interrupt their partner. If you're having trouble coming up with your own safety plan, call The Hotline for help.
  • Note, above all, that helping your coworker really doesn't matter what could make a difference. Respect their decisions – maybe you do not know all the factors involved. You will not have your coworker doing what you want, or expect them to do. Instead of focusing on being the one to solve the problem for them, focus on being helpful and responsive when needed.
