Finding Closure After Abuse

Going on after any breakup is daunting but it can be especially difficult to recover after an abusive relationship. All breakups may have their aftermath of sadness and loss, but the fallout may include continued harassment or attacks for someone transitioning from victim to survivor. The subsequent mental distress and emotional stress may raise a survivor 's question, "Is it really worth leaving?

Leaving Is Worth It

How is it so difficult to move on after an abuse? Abuse has its roots in power and control and an abusive spouse retains that power by undermining the self-esteem of their victim and destroying their spirit. When you end an abusive relationship, repairing your life can be a daunting process but it is possible to find closure and peace with time and space. A life without violence awaits you, and you are so worth it.

Cutting off touch with Partner

You can feel the need to offer forgiveness during the healing process, support your abusive partner through the break up, or show them how well you're doing. It's hard to get closure though without severing all ties with your ex. To stop contact with your ex partner try various approaches. Enter their telephone number and change your name. When you are picking up the phone to dial, place the handset in another room and walk away — or just dial the hotline.

Resist the temptation that social media views them up. Unfriendly or block them and if pictures or news keep popping up, removing mutual friends might be helpful as well.

Try to write a letter with all the things you want to tell your ex, and don't send it — or send it to your therapist if you're in counseling.

Surround Yourself With Support

Let yourself be given help and support from others after an abusive relationship. Spend some time with family and friends who care about you. Tell them what you need from them, whether it's someone to speak to you about what you've been through, or someone to prevent you from answering your ex phone calls, stop you from getting them back, and so on.

When your abusive partner isolates you from friends and family, you can feel that you don't have the support network anymore-but there are still people who want to help. Try seeking a professional to meet with, or joining a community group. If you call the hotline you can be connected to services in your area by one of our advocates.

You Will Get Better By Time

The old saying "time heals all wounds" may be painfully irritating, but it does contain reality. It does take time and space to recover. Allow yourself the time you need to recover.

Recovery is different for everyone and they have to figure out what works for them.

Consider Counseling

If you feel therapy might be helpful, it's always better to do it sooner. Therapy can be helpful to all, as it is a place where you can learn to increase your self-awareness, articulate your priorities and look at the opportunities that are before you. Counseling sessions provide patients with a supportive and confidential environment to share their emotions, opinions and fears. Professionals are non-judgmental third-party professionals who listen and are able to help patients work with the problems they encounter.

Advising doesn't necessarily mean you 're mentally ill or can't cope on your own. Therapy is about how much you put in order to help yourself recover and thrive. Talking to a trauma specialist can help survivors address their remaining anxiety and find ways to alleviate that stress. Such professionals can help handle painful memories or events so we can move forward. Often, they can help survivors learn to control their powerful emotions such as fear and rage.

A strong match between therapist and client in a therapeutic partnership is one of the most important healing factors. Look for someone who will make you feel heard, understood, comfortable and safe.
