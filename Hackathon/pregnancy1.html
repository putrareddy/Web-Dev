Pregnancy & Abuse : Safety During PostPartum

To new parents the time immediately after childbirth may be incredibly joyful. Sometimes too, coping with a new baby 's treatment and adjusting your lifestyle to what that means is daunting. This time is also a cycle of increased tension and risk for parents who have an abusive partner. Several research have shown that witnessing violence is a risk factor for postpartum depression and other postpartum mental health problems, so reporting emotional, verbal and physical violence experiences with your maternal health care professional could be beneficial because they can help you identify postpartum prevention steps. You may also want to start studying signs and supports.

After birth the body will still be physically rejusting. You'll need a time of healing before engaging in sexual intercourse after your body goes through childbirth. Depending on your birth experience, your doctor , nurse, or midwife may advise you regarding this length of time. Your abusive partner can attempt to reassert power and control by discarding or downplaying these suggestions with guilt, threats or even forcing sex before you're ready. Such habits are sexual harassment, which may cause you health issues or an prolonged period of healing.Contacting your healthcare provider or a domestic violence program about these incidents may allow you to create a safety plan to increase your sexual and physical safety during this period. Examples of strategies you may use could include:

  • A support person who stays in your home during your healing period;
  • Staying with your baby at home or shelter of a loving family member or friend when you are healing;
  • Adapt your bedtime to occasions when your partner is away from home;
  • If you're afraid that your partner is trying to get you pregnant again, find secure and undetectable forms of contraception that don't interfere with your child's option of eating.

Like always, you do the best of your situation, and these suggestions are not guidelines, just ideas for future exploration if you think your protection could be improved.

As advocates we use Power and Control Wheels tools to discuss various kinds of abuse. There is also a different Power and Control Wheel for pregnancy and postpartum periods. Using Children is one of the sections on the original wheel, and those strategies can be especially impactful during this time. It's normal to have new parents discuss their child-raising desires with each other. In an abusive situation the abusive partner can neglect, circumvent or manipulate the wishes and concerns of the other parent.

How to feed your baby is one place where this can come up. Many parents may choose to breastfeed, while others may opt to use formula to feed their infant. To move forward with each of these approaches it is very important to feel comfortable having the support of your partner. Breastfeeding has many advantages and can improve your child's bond and also help to reduce the impacts of mental health problems postpartum. Yet for certain parents it can also be mentally and emotionally exhausting.If your partner belittles you for challenges that you have with breastfeeding, prevents you from having time to breastfeed or pump or pressures you to breastfeed without providing support, these may be red flags for abuse.

The place around your baby's sleep is where you can experience this. There are several hypotheses and approaches to help children (and their parents!) sleep. You should expect to make decisions about how to respond to your child when they wake up, where to make the sleeping area for your child, what makes a healthy sleeping environment and who is going to respond to the child. When your partner prevents you from having a regular sleep schedule, it actively starts fighting near the sleeping area of the child, forbids you from soothing your child or fails to intervene when the child wakes up, these could be red flags for violence.

There are many sources of support for new parents, and connecting with them can help get a glimpse of your new role and how best to deal with words and actions about your partner. Your pediatrician or postpartum care physician might have knowledge about support groups for new parents and their kids, so calling their office to find some resources may help. Social networking and parenting websites such as Baby Center, Parenting, The Change, and What to Expect have fora where you can reach out to other parents and sometimes even find local groups and resources in your area.

If one-on-one counseling is more suited to your needs, you may want to consider reaching out to a doula postpartum. A postpartum doula is helping parents acclimatize to their new positions. They can provide support and education for breastfeeding, as well as other skills that improve bonding between parents and babies to improve self-confidence among parents.
